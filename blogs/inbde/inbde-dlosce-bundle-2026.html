The 2026 INBDE and DLOSCE Pricing Bundle: Costs, Rules, and State Acceptance

The JCNDE's $1,080 combined pricing bundle saves applicants $470 compared to buying the cognitive and clinical exams separately, but with only eight state dental boards currently accepting the DLOSCE, strategic career planning is essential before locking in your testing pathway.

Quick Answers

What is the INBDE and DLOSCE pricing bundle?

The Joint Commission on National Dental Examinations (JCNDE) implemented a testing package that allows candidates to purchase both the written INBDE and the objective clinical DLOSCE simultaneously for $1,080, significantly reducing the financial burden of licensure.

What is the exact financial savings in 2026?

For the 2026 testing cycle, purchasing the bundle saves candidates $470 compared to the standalone costs. The individual fees are $890 for the INBDE and $660 for the DLOSCE, totaling $1,550 if purchased entirely separately.

How do retroactive refunds work if I apply for the exams separately?

If you submit separate applications for both the INBDE and the DLOSCE within the same testing window, the Department of Testing Services automatically applies a retroactive $470 refund to your DLOSCE application within five business days of both being approved. No manual petition is required.

Which state dental boards accept the DLOSCE in 2026?

Eight states formally accept the DLOSCE to fulfill clinical examination requirements: Alaska, Arizona, Colorado, Indiana, Iowa, Kentucky, Oregon, and Washington. In specific jurisdictions like Alaska and Iowa, the DLOSCE may only fulfill partial clinical requirements.

Are international candidates eligible for the pricing bundle?

Yes, but international candidates from non-CODA accredited programs must pay an additional, non-refundable $435 processing fee and obtain a formal Course-by-Course evaluation report from Educational Credential Evaluators (ECE) prior to application.

1. The Economic Architecture of the 2026 Pricing Bundle

The landscape of dental licensure in the United States is undergoing a profound methodological and economic transformation. Historically, candidates faced a fragmented and exorbitant assessment pipeline, requiring a written cognitive examination followed by a live-patient clinical examination administered by regional testing agencies. To accelerate the adoption of modernized, non-patient-based clinical testing, the JCNDE introduced the INBDE and DLOSCE pricing bundle.

This financial intervention explicitly links the primary written examination (INBDE) with the new computerized clinical examination (DLOSCE). According to the official 2026 Candidate Guides, the standalone fee for the INBDE is $890, while the DLOSCE is priced at $660. Purchasing these assessments independently incurs a total cost of $1,550. The JCNDE pricing bundle reduces this aggregate cost to $1,080, generating a direct capital savings of $470 for the candidate.

The bundle is engineered to restructure candidate behavior. By lowering the financial barrier to entry for the DLOSCE, the ADA and JCNDE are incentivizing the next generation of practitioners to bypass traditional live-patient exams, thereby increasing pressure on non-adopting state dental boards to modernize their statutes.

2. Transactional Mechanics and Retroactive Refunds

Recognizing that candidate testing timelines do not always align perfectly, the Department of Testing Services integrated a sophisticated retroactive refund mechanism for the bundle. Candidates are not forced to purchase both examinations in a single, simultaneous transaction to capture the discount.

If a candidate strategically phases their testing schedule—perhaps applying for the INBDE during their third year of dental school and the DLOSCE during their fourth year—they remain eligible for the cost reduction, provided both administrations occur within the designated programmatic windows. Once the DENTPIN system registers that both standalone applications have been approved and processed, the JCNDE automatically retroactively applies a $470 credit to the candidate's DLOSCE ledger.

This systematic audit operates independently of the candidate; the refund is initiated within five business days and requires no formal appeals or secondary paperwork. However, candidates must be aware that examination fees themselves remain strictly non-refundable and non-transferable under all other circumstances.

Ancillary and Administrative Surcharges

The $1,080 bundle covers the examinations and reporting to three selected state boards. Deviations incur penalties: additional results reports cost $55 each, and a formal psychometric audit of your examination score carries a $105 fee if requested within 30 days of score release.

3. Jurisdictional Acceptance: Where the DLOSCE Operates in 2026

The ultimate utility of the pricing bundle is entirely dependent on the legislative framework of the specific jurisdiction in which the candidate intends to practice. The United States dental licensure system remains a fractured patchwork of sovereign state boards, and universal acceptance of the DLOSCE has not yet materialized.

As of 2026, the ADA Dental Licensure Dashboard and the JCNDE confirm that exactly eight states have adopted regulations permitting the use of the DLOSCE for clinical licensure.

4. Comparative Economics: The Traditional Clinical Testing Burden

To accurately contextualize the magnitude of the $470 bundle discount, it is necessary to analyze the prohibitive economic ecosystem of legacy clinical testing. Candidates forced by jurisdictional constraints to bypass the DLOSCE routinely face catastrophic financial outlays.

The predominant alternative to the DLOSCE is the ADEX (American Board of Dental Examiners) examination series, administered by agencies such as the CDCA-WREB-CITA. According to published 2026 fee schedules, the base examination fee for the ADEX Dental Series is $2,795. This fee encompasses the restorative, endodontic, and prosthodontic components, along with a computerized DSE OSCE. Notably, as of August 1, 2026, the Periodontal Examination has been permanently reinstated as a required clinical component, eliminating prior exemptions.

The financial bleeding does not stop at the base fee. The traditional ADEX framework forces candidates to absorb multiple ancillary costs: specialized typodonts incur a fixed cost of $150 per procedure, and host universities levy variable facility fees that can exceed a thousand dollars. Furthermore, while the initial fee covers one complimentary retake per procedure, subsequent failures introduce a $1,295 penalty per clinical section.

A candidate utilizing the JCNDE bundle achieves total licensure assessment compliance for a predictable $1,080. A candidate navigating the ADEX pathway faces the unbundled INBDE ($890) plus the ADEX base fee ($2,795) and typodonts, pushing the baseline expenditure well beyond $4,000.

5. International Dentists and Credentialing Premiums

The procedural matrix grows substantially more complex for international dentists and candidates attending programs not accredited by the Commission on Dental Accreditation (CODA). For these individuals, the pricing bundle remains fully accessible, but it is heavily front-loaded with mandatory credentialing prerequisites.

All non-CODA candidates are subjected to a strict international processing fee of $435. This surcharge is levied by the Department of Testing Services to offset the administrative overhead associated with verifying foreign credentials and managing international testing logistics. This fee is independent of the bundle; thus, a non-CODA candidate faces a baseline aggregate cost of $1,515.

Before an international candidate can generate a DENTPIN and secure the bundle, their educational degrees must undergo rigorous verification by Educational Credential Evaluators (ECE). The JCNDE strongly recommends procuring a U.S. Course by Course Report ($199), as it fulfills the ADA mandate while simultaneously satisfying the application requirements for virtually all U.S. advanced standing dental programs. Candidates currently enrolled as students in non-CODA programs must also secure a formalized Certification of Eligibility Form, bearing the embossed seal of their university and the signature of the dean, and submit it directly to the JCNDE.

Strict DENTPIN Nomenclature Alignment

Precision in data entry is critical during the credentialing phase. The name registered on the ECE evaluation report must match the candidate's DENTPIN registry exactly. Discrepancies as minor as an unhyphenated surname will trigger application rejection and prolonged bureaucratic delays.

6. Psychometric Synergies and Examination Modalities

The pairing of the INBDE and the DLOSCE is not merely a financial strategy; it is an alignment of psychometric methodologies. Historically, candidates partitioned their study efforts, treating the written cognitive exam and the live-patient clinical exam as isolated academic events. The integration of these modern testing paradigms renders such partitioning obsolete.

The INBDE tests foundational knowledge through integrated, case-based item formats. Day 2 of the INBDE is exclusively dedicated to complex patient cases, presenting medical histories, odontograms, and radiographs. This architecture is functionally identical to the baseline cognitive mechanics of the DLOSCE.

The DLOSCE expands upon this reasoning by subjecting the candidate to high-fidelity simulated environments. Over the course of 6 hours and 45 minutes, candidates evaluate sophisticated 3D computerized models to determine precise restorative margins, write complex pharmacological prescriptions, and execute advanced treatment planning without the uncontrollable variables of live human subjects.

Because both examinations rely heavily on clinical vignettes and deductive diagnostic reasoning, the cognitive load required to master the material overlaps significantly. The bundle effectively incentivizes candidates to schedule these examinations in closer temporal proximity, leveraging peak knowledge retention across both formats.

7. Scheduling Regulations, Penalties, and the 2026 Practice Analysis

The financial protections afforded by the pricing bundle can be rapidly eroded by candidate negligence regarding Prometric scheduling parameters. The JCNDE enforces an aggressively tiered penalty system to protect its limited inventory of secure testing seats.

Saturdays and Sundays are strictly excluded from the calculation of business days. Candidates who reschedule 30 or more business days before their test date incur a $50 fee. This penalty escalates to $70 if rescheduled between 5 and 29 business days, and $150 if altered within 1 to 4 business days of the appointment.

Catastrophic scheduling errors yield absolute financial forfeiture. If a candidate fails to appear, or arrives more than 30 minutes after their scheduled start time, they are categorically refused admission. In the context of the bundle, forfeiting the DLOSCE appointment permanently burns the $660 allocated value, requiring a new application entirely. Furthermore, candidates agree to binding arbitration as a prerequisite to testing, limiting legal recourse for alleged examination irregularities.

The landscape will continue to evolve. The JCNDE is executing a comprehensive dental practice analysis in 2026 to update the test specifications for both the INBDE and the DLOSCE. Subject matter expert panels will convene in 2027 to review these findings and potentially recommend new performance standards. Candidates exploiting the current pricing bundle must recognize that while the $1,080 footprint represents peak economic efficiency, the psychometric rigorousness required to clear these hurdles is engineered to intensify in direct correlation with the advancing complexities of modern dentistry.
