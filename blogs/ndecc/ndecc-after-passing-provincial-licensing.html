NDECC After Passing – Complete Provincial Licensing Guide 2026

Your NDEB Certificate proves national competence. But to legally treat patients, you need a licence from the dental regulatory authority in the province or territory where you intend to work.

Quick Answers

Can I practise anywhere in Canada after I receive my NDEB Certificate?

No. The NDEB Certificate demonstrates you meet the national standard of competence, but it does not allow you to practise dentistry. You must apply for a licence from the Dental Regulatory Authority (DRA) in the specific province or territory where you intend to work.

What documents do I need to apply for a provincial dental licence?

You typically need your NDEB Certificate, official dental degree transcripts, government-issued ID, a certificate of standing from any jurisdiction where you have previously been licensed, jurisprudence examination results, and proof of language proficiency if required.

What is a jurisprudence exam?

A jurisprudence exam tests your knowledge of the local laws, ethics, and regulations that govern the practice of dentistry in a particular province. Most provinces require you to pass this exam before you can be licensed.

How much does it cost to get a provincial dental licence in 2026?

Costs vary by province. In Alberta, the application fee is $500 and the registration fee is $1,000. In New Brunswick, the 2026 licensing fee is $3,890.03. Expect to budget $1,500–$3,000 for initial licensing fees, plus annual renewal fees.

Do I need professional liability insurance to practise in Canada?

Yes. Every province requires dentists to carry professional liability insurance. In Ontario, coverage of up to $2 million is included in your annual registration fee. In Alberta, liability insurance is included in the Annual Practice Permit Fee of $2,100.

1. NDEB Certification vs Provincial Licensure – What Is the Difference?

Many internationally trained dentists mistakenly believe that passing the NDECC and receiving the NDEB Certificate is the end of the licensing journey. It is not. It is the final step of the national certification process and the first step of the provincial licensing process.

The NDEB Certificate is a national credential. It confirms that you have met the national standard of competence expected of a beginning dental practitioner in Canada. However, healthcare regulation in Canada is provincial. The NDEB Certificate alone does not grant you the legal authority to treat patients.

Provincial dental regulatory authorities (DRAs) issue the actual licence to practise. You must apply to the DRA in the province or territory where you intend to work. Only after the DRA reviews your credentials, confirms your NDEB Certificate, verifies your identity and background, and approves your application will you receive a licence.

The NDEB explicitly states: “Having an NDEB certificate does not allow you to practice in Canada until you apply for and are granted a license in a specific province/territory.” The NDEB Certificate is a prerequisite for provincial licensure, not a substitute for it.

The Two-Step Reality

Step 1: NDEB Certification (AFK → ACJ → NDECC). Step 2: Provincial Licensure (apply to the DRA in your chosen province). You cannot skip Step 2. You cannot practise in Quebec with an Ontario licence. You must hold a licence from the DRA where your practice is located.

2. The General Provincial Licensure Process – Step by Step

While each province has its own specific requirements, the general process for obtaining a provincial dental licence is consistent across Canada.

Step 1 – Obtain NDEB Certification

You must successfully complete the NDEB Equivalency Process (AFK, ACJ, and NDECC) and receive your official NDEB Certificate. This is a mandatory prerequisite for every province and territory.

Step 2 – Choose a Province or Territory

Decide where you wish to practise. Consider factors such as licensure requirements, cost of living, job market, language requirements (English or French), and immigration status. Familiarise yourself with the specific requirements of that region‘s DRA.

Step 3 – Submit Your Application

Complete the licensure application for your chosen DRA. Submit the required documentation and pay the associated fees. Applications are typically submitted online through the DRA’s applicant portal.

Step 4 – Complete the Jurisprudence and Ethics Examination

Most provinces require candidates to pass an examination on local laws, ethics, and regulations. This exam tests your knowledge of the specific provincial legislation that governs the practice of dentistry in that jurisdiction.

Step 5 – Obtain Professional Liability Insurance

Every province requires dentists to carry malpractice insurance. In some provinces, the insurance is included in your annual registration fee. In others, you must purchase it separately.

Step 6 – Receive Your Licence

Once your documents are approved, you pass the jurisprudence exam, and all fees are paid, the DRA will issue your certificate of registration. You are now legally authorised to practise dentistry in that province.

Plan for 3–6 Months of Processing Time

The provincial licensing process is not instant. From submitting your application to receiving your licence, you should budget 3–6 months. Delays occur due to document verification, jurisprudence exam scheduling, and criminal record checks. Start the process as soon as you receive your NDEB Certificate.

3. Required Documentation – What You Need to Have Ready

Provincial DRAs require a standard set of documents. Prepare these in advance to avoid delays.

Core documents required by most DRAs:

Document Notes
NDEB Certificate Official certificate from the National Dental Examining Board of Canada
Dental degree and transcripts Official transcripts from your dental education program; certified translations if not in English or French
Government-issued photo ID Passport, driver’s licence, or permanent resident card
Certificate of Standing Required if you have been previously licensed elsewhere; the regulatory body must send it directly to the DRA
Jurisprudence examination results Proof of successful completion, if applicable
Language proficiency test results IELTS or CELPIP for English; TEF for French (required in Quebec and some other provinces)
Criminal record check Mandatory in every province; some DRAs initiate the check on your behalf

The NDEB provides a list of provincial and territorial DRAs on its Contacts and Organisations page. You are encouraged to contact the DRA where you intend to practise to confirm specific licensing requirements.

4. Ontario – Licensing Through the RCDSO

The Royal College of Dental Surgeons of Ontario (RCDSO) is the regulatory authority for dentists in Ontario. Ontario is Canada’s largest dental market, but it also has one of the most competitive licensing environments.

Step 1 – NDEB Certification

Before applying to RCDSO, all internationally educated dentists must be certified by the NDEB. You must complete the NDEB Equivalency Process (for graduates of non-accredited programs).

Step 2 – Submit Application to RCDSO

Submit your application through the RCDSO online portal. Required documents include:

- NDEB Certificate
- Proof of identity (passport)
- Academic transcripts and dental degree
- Certificate of Standing if previously licensed elsewhere
- Proof of English or French language proficiency (if required)

Step 3 – Jurisprudence and Ethics Training

RCDSO requires mandatory jurisprudence and ethics training for all international applicants. The training covers Ontario’s dental laws, professional standards, and ethical obligations.

Step 4 – Professional Liability Insurance

Ontario has a unique Professional Liability Program (PLP). There is no premium – the cost of PLP protection is included in annual RCDSO registration fees. Anyone eligible for protection is entitled to be indemnified for up to $2 million in damages and legal costs.

Ontario – No Separate Insurance Purchase Required

Unlike most other provinces, Ontario includes professional liability insurance in your annual registration fees. You do not need to purchase a separate policy. The PLP provides up to $2 million in coverage for dental negligence claims.

5. British Columbia – Licensing Through the BCCOHP

In British Columbia, the regulatory authority for dentists is the BC College of Oral Health Professionals (BCCOHP), formerly known as the College of Dental Surgeons of British Columbia (CDSBC).

Step 1 – NDEB Certification

To apply for registration and practise as a dentist in BC, you must complete the national examination administered by the NDEB. If your dental training was obtained outside of accredited jurisdictions (Canada, US, Australia, New Zealand, Ireland), you must successfully complete either an accredited two-year Qualifying Program or the NDEB Equivalency Process.

Step 2 – Submit Application to BCCOHP

Once you have successfully completed the appropriate NDEB process and received your NDEB certificate, you can apply for registration through the BCCOHP.

Step 3 – Criminal Record Check

A mandatory Criminal Record Check must be completed through the BC Ministry of Public Safety and Solicitor General. BCCOHP will automatically submit the check during your application process.

Step 4 – Jurisprudence Examination

BC requires a jurisprudence examination that tests knowledge of BC’s dental laws, ethics, and regulations.

6. Alberta – Licensing Through the CDSA

The College of Dental Surgeons of Alberta (CDSA) regulates the dental profession in Alberta. Alberta has introduced new pathways to increase flexibility for internationally trained dentists.

Step 1 – Obtain NDEB Certification

Before applying to CDSA, you must be certified by the NDEB through the Equivalency Process (for graduates of non-accredited international programs).

Step 2 – Apply to CDSA for Licensure

Submit your application through the CDSA Applicant Portal. Required documents include:

- NDEB Certificate
- Certified dental degree and official transcripts
- Valid government-issued photo ID
- Certificates of Standing from every regulatory body where you have been licensed (issued within 8 weeks)
- Language proficiency test results (if applicable)

Fees: Application Fee – $500 CAD; Registration Fee – $1,000 CAD.

Step 3 – Complete the Alberta Ethics and Jurisprudence Exam

All applicants must complete this online exam covering:

- Alberta’s Health Professions Act
- CDSA Standards of Practice
- Patient rights, informed consent, and ethics
- Legal scope of practice in Alberta

Step 4 – Professional Liability Insurance

All dentists in Alberta must carry professional liability insurance. The insurance is included in your Annual Practice Permit Fee of $2,100 CAD (pro-rated after July 1). No additional insurance purchase is required.

Alternative Licensure Pathways in Alberta (2026)

Pathway Details
Supportive Pathway Pilot Program (2025 Intake) Restricted practice license under supervision for IEDs who have passed AFK and ACJ. Practice reversible dental procedures under a supervising dentist. 1-year pilot program with 12 seats. Application deadline: June 20, 2025
Bridge to Dental Practice in Canada (BTDPC) 8-month clinical and communication training program offered by ACFD at University of Alberta, Dalhousie University, and Université Laval. Must have passed AFK and ACJ and be a PR/citizen. Leads to eligibility for NDECC and licensure

Alberta’s New Supportive Pathway – A Game Changer

If you have passed the AFK and ACJ but are still working on the NDECC, Alberta now offers a restricted practice licence under supervision. You can treat patients under a supervising dentist while completing your NDECC. This is the first program of its kind in Canada and significantly reduces the income gap during the licensing process.

7. Quebec – Licensing Through the Ordre des dentistes du Québec

Quebec has distinct requirements, including mandatory French language proficiency. The regulatory authority is the Ordre des dentistes du Québec (ODQ).

Step 1 – Complete the NDEB Equivalency Process

Internationally trained dentists must first complete the NDEB Equivalency Process. Once the equivalency process is successfully completed, you may register for the Order‘s examination.

Step 2 – Submit Your Equivalency Application

Submit the “Demande de reconnaissance d‘équivalence de diplôme ou de formation” form to the ODQ with the following documents:

- University academic record (course descriptions, credits, grades)
- Certified copy of your dental degree
- Certified copy of your NDEB Certificate or proof of success
- CV attesting to work experience and continuing education
- Certified translation of documents not in French or English
- Payment of administrative fees

Applications are evaluated every two months.

Step 3 – French Language Proficiency

In Quebec, candidates from all professional orders must prove their ability to work in French. You must demonstrate French proficiency before you can be licensed.

Step 4 – Professional Liability Insurance

In Quebec, you must subscribe to the professional liability insurance fund of the Ordre des dentistes du Québec (FARPODQ). You must pay the required fees and subscribe to the Fund.

Quebec’s French Requirement Is Non-Negotiable

If you do not speak French, you cannot be licensed in Quebec. The province requires proof of French proficiency through standardised testing (TEF or TCF). Plan your language preparation accordingly if Quebec is your target province.

8. New Brunswick – Licensing Through the NBDent

The New Brunswick Dental Society (NBDent) regulates dentists in New Brunswick. The province has transparent fee structures and is welcoming to internationally trained dentists.

Licensing Process:

1. Complete the NDEB Equivalency Process and receive your NDEB Certificate
2. Complete the online application form through the NBDent website
3. Submit your NDEB Certificate (new graduates may submit an official letter)
4. Specialists must provide proof of having successfully completed the National Dental Specialty Examination (NDSE)

2026 Licensing Fees:

The 2026 licensing fee in New Brunswick is $3,890.03. This includes:

- One-time registration fee: $150.00
- Annual license fee: $3,167.90
- CDA membership fee: $497.50 + HST

10. Common Requirements Across All Provinces

While each province has unique elements, the following requirements are universal across Canada.

Jurisprudence Examination

Most provinces require a jurisprudence and ethics examination that tests knowledge related to local laws, ethics, and regulation of the profession. These exams are typically computer-based, multiple-choice, and require a passing score of 70–75%.

Criminal Record Check

Dentists are required to undergo a criminal record check in every province. Under British Columbia’s Criminal Records Review Act, dentists are among the regulated professionals who must submit to checks due to their work with children and vulnerable persons. Other provinces have similar requirements.

Language Proficiency

Some provinces require proof of proficiency in English or French. Quebec requires French proficiency. Ontario and British Columbia may require English proficiency depending on your educational background.

Continuing Education Requirements

Most provinces mandate ongoing professional development to maintain licensure. Review the specific continuing education (CE) requirements set by your provincial authority.

Alberta requires regulated members to complete 100 hours of continuing education in five-year cycles. Of these 100 hours, 10 may be taken to promote personal or non-clinical development. Other provinces have similar requirements, typically ranging from 60 to 100 credits per cycle.

Professional Liability Insurance

Every province requires dentists to carry malpractice insurance. Coverage limits vary:

Province Insurance Requirement Coverage Limit
Ontario Included in RCDSO registration fees Up to $2 million
Alberta Included in Annual Practice Permit Fee ($2,100) Minimum requirements met by CDSA program
Quebec Must subscribe to FARPODQ As set by the Fund
Other provinces Separate purchase required Typically $1–3 million per occurrence

Start Your Provincial Application Immediately

The day you receive your NDEB Certificate, start your provincial licensing application. Do not wait. The process takes 3–6 months. In that time, you can research job opportunities, secure a practice location, and arrange accommodation. Every month of delay is lost income.
